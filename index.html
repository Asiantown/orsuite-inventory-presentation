Dual-Sourcing Inventory Benchmark

How do PPO and DQN behave when one product can be ordered from a fast expensive supplier or a slower cheaper supplier before demand is known?

1 · Decision problem

The inventory decision

Each period, the policy sees current inventory and outstanding orders, then chooses how much to request from each supplier before random customer demand arrives.

Observe

Current net inventory and every order already moving through the two supply pipelines.

→
Place two orders

[fast, regular], with each quantity from 0 through 4. That gives 25 possible order pairs.

→
Resolve the period

Deliveries arrive, demand is sampled, and the system pays ordering plus holding or backlog cost.

Model at a glance

ComponentIn this benchmark
StateFour pipeline positions plus current net inventory.
ActionOne order quantity for each supplier.
DemandAn independent integer from 0 through 4 each period.
ObjectiveReward is negative total cost, so maximizing reward minimizes ordering, holding, and backlog cost over 100 periods.

2 · Scenario

The benchmark scenario

The faster source costs more, the regular source requires planning ahead, and shortages are deliberately much more expensive than extra stock.

ParameterValueInterpretation
Lead times[1, 3]Fast supply arrives after 1 period; regular supply after 3.
Incremental order costs[10, 0]The fast source carries a 10-unit premium.
Holding cost5Cost per positive inventory unit per period.
Backlog cost95Cost per unfilled demand unit per period.
DemandUniform{0,1,2,3,4}Mean demand is 2 units per period.
Maximum order4 per supplierEach period's action is one of 25 order pairs.
Episode length100One evaluation episode represents 100 inventory periods.
Initial stateEmpty pipeline, zero net inventoryNo inventory or outstanding orders at reset.

Adapted from the public dual-sourcing instance of Böttcher, Asikis, and Fragkos (2023). Its 0/2-period lead times become 1/3 here because the current ORSuite environment requires positive lead times.

3 · Learning algorithms

Two ways to learn the ordering policy

PPO and DQN learn differently, but both keep access to every one of the 25 feasible order pairs.

PPO

Proximal Policy Optimization learns a policy directly and can use the native MultiDiscrete([5, 5]) action.

Action: [fast order, regular order]

DQN

Deep Q-Network learns a value for each action. A reversible wrapper maps integers 0–24 to the same order pairs.

Decode: a → [a // 5, a % 5]

Same feasible decisions

The wrapper is one-to-one: action 0 maps to [0, 0] and action 24 maps to [4, 4]. DQN changes the representation, not the inventory choices.

Training rewards are divided by 100 for numerical conditioning; evaluation always returns to the original unscaled inventory costs.

4 · Evaluation design

A controlled comparison

Training varies across seeds, while checkpoint evaluation holds the demand scenarios and scoring rule fixed.

Training seeds30, 1, and 2
Checkpoints5roughly every 10k timesteps
Held-out episodes20100 periods each
ControlImplementationWhy it matters
Training50,000 requested timesteps per algorithm and seedOne uninterrupted learning run per seed.
Held-out demandThe same seeded scenarios at every checkpointChanges in the curve come from the learned policy, not a changing test set.
Action selectionDeterministic during evaluationRemoves extra sampling noise from the comparison.
MetricMean unscaled cost per periodLower is better and remains interpretable in the original model units.

Inventory reference: a tailored-base-surge policy with regular base order 1 and fast order-up-to target 7 was calibrated on a separate demand stream, then scored on the same held-out scenarios. It is a structured heuristic, not another ML model.

6 · Interpretation

What this run shows, and what comes next

This is a compact integration benchmark under one controlled inventory scenario, not a final ranking of PPO and DQN.

What this run establishes

  • The migrated environment passes Gymnasium validation.
  • PPO and DQN can train against the same feasible inventory decisions.
  • Held-out evaluation produces reproducible learning curves and saved artifacts.

What I would test next

  • Use more seeds and longer training budgets.
  • Tune PPO and DQN separately under the same evaluation rules.
  • Test a more compact observation representation and stronger inventory-specific policies.

Current scope: one product, two suppliers, synthetic demand, normalized costs, three training seeds, and no extensive hyperparameter search.
